## 1. USCIS Immigration Fees and Related Procedures Required by H.R.1 Reconciliation Bill (Interim Final Rule)

**Source:** Federal Register / DHS / USCIS — https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/29/2026-08333/uscis-immigration-fees-and-related-procedures-required-by-hr1-reconciliation-bill
**Published:** 2026-04-29
**Effective date:** 2026-05-29
**Document #:** 2026-08333

### What changed
This interim final rule implements fee provisions mandated by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (H.R.1). Key changes affecting Messenginfo users: (1) TPS-based EADs are now capped at 1 year per grant or renewal, or the remaining duration of the TPS designation, whichever is shorter. USCIS will no longer auto-extend TPS EADs beyond one year via Federal Register notices. (2) TPS beneficiaries must actively renew Form I-765 each year. (3) The H.R.1 TPS EAD fee (set July 2025) is non-waivable and non-reducible by statute — the existing I-912 fee waiver applies only to the pre-H.R.1 USCIS filing fees, not the new add-on fees. (4) A new $100 minimum annual asylum application fee and $100 annual asylum maintenance fee are imposed.

### What was known before
TPS EADs were routinely auto-extended for 6–12 months via Federal Register notices. Ukraine TPS EADs were most recently auto-extended through April 19, 2026. The prior fee structure allowed fee waivers (I-912) for most TPS-related filings.

### Affected Messenginfo services
- **TPS renewal section / FAQ** — Must be updated to reflect mandatory annual I-765 renewal requirement and end of auto-extensions
- **Pricing/fee information pages** — Must clarify that H.R.1 TPS EAD fees cannot be waived even with I-912
- **I-765 instructions page** — Must add guidance about 1-year EAD cap
- **User-facing copy** — Any references to auto-extension of EADs must be removed or corrected

### Recommended action
- **HIGH PRIORITY:** Update TPS section and I-765 instructions before May 29, 2026 effective date
- Add alert banner to TPS section explaining the annual renewal requirement
- Update fee waiver (I-912) guidance to distinguish waivable vs. non-waivable fees

### Risk level
**HIGH** — Effective May 29, 2026. Directly impacts every Ukrainian TPS holder's EAD renewal workflow. Failure to update could cause users to miss renewal deadlines or incorrectly assume auto-extension.

## 2. USCIS Will Grant Adjustment of Status Only in Extraordinary Circumstances (Policy Memo)

**Source:** USCIS Newsroom — https://www.uscis.gov/newsroom/news-releases/us-citizenship-and-immigration-services-will-grant-adjustment-of-status-only-in-extraordinary
**Published:** 2026-05-22
**Effective date:** 2026-05-21 (policy memo PM-602-0199)
**Document #:** PM-602-0199-AdjustmentOfStatusAndDiscretion-20260521

### What changed
USCIS issued a policy memo directing that adjustment of status (AOS) will only be granted in extraordinary circumstances. Going forward, aliens in the U.S. temporarily who want a Green Card must return to their home country to apply through consular processing via the Department of State, except where the applicant demonstrates unusual or outstanding equities. Officers are directed to assess all relevant factors on a case-by-case basis.

### What was known before
Adjustment of status has historically been available to eligible applicants present in the U.S. who were otherwise eligible for an immigrant visa, without requiring departure. This was widely used by TPS holders, parolees, and other nonimmigrants.

### Affected Messenginfo services
- **FAQ / general immigration info** — Should note that AOS is now extremely restricted
- **TPS section** — Ukrainian TPS holders considering Green Card pathways need to understand this limitation
- **Translation service** — Users preparing AOS packages (I-485) should understand the changed landscape

### Recommended action
- Add informational note to relevant FAQ sections about the AOS policy change
- Do NOT provide legal advice on whether specific users qualify for "extraordinary circumstances"
- Consider adding a general information banner about this change

### Risk level
**MEDIUM** — Affects users considering long-term immigration pathways. No immediate deadline but significant strategic impact on TPS holders planning Green Card applications.

## 3. Signatures on Immigration Benefit Requests (Interim Final Rule)

**Source:** Federal Register / DHS / USCIS — https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/11/2026-09289/signatures-on-immigration-benefit-requests
**Published:** 2026-05-11
**Effective date:** 2026-07-10
**Document #:** 2026-09289

### What changed
This interim final rule codifies USCIS authority to either reject or deny a benefit request when an invalid signature is discovered after intake. Previously, policy allowed denial for bad signatures, but it was not in the regulations. Under the new rule: (1) Invalid signatures (copy-pasted images, stamped, signed by attorney/preparer, or generated by signature software) can result in denial — meaning the filing fee is lost. (2) A denial preserves I-290B appeal rights, whereas a rejection does not. (3) No "cure" of a deficient signature is permitted. (4) Forms N-600 and N-600K are exempt. All other benefit requests, including I-765, I-821, I-131, and I-912, are covered.

### What was known before
Since 2018, USCIS policy (not regulation) allowed denial for deficient signatures. The new rule makes this regulatory, removing any ambiguity.

### Affected Messenginfo services
- **Form preparation guidance** — Must emphasize genuine handwritten (wet ink) signatures
- **I-765, I-821, I-131 instructions** — Add warning about signature requirements
- **User-facing copy** — Warn against digital signature tools, attorney/preparer signatures on applicant signature lines

### Recommended action
- Update all form preparation pages to include clear signature guidance before July 10, 2026
- Add prominent warning: digital/electronic signatures, stamps, or preparer signatures on applicant lines will result in denial and loss of filing fee
- No immediate action needed (effective July 10)

### Risk level
**MEDIUM** — Effective July 10, 2026. Users who habitually use digital signature tools or have preparers sign on their behalf risk denial and lost fees. Update within 2–3 weeks.
